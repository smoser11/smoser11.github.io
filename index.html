Travel to Mousehole

Day 1

On the seven-hour train ride:

  1. Overheard: (young girl) “May I have cheese, please?”; (reply inaudible); (young girl) “I DON’T LIKE CHEESE!”
  2. Last leg of trip was 3 hours on a fully packed train. I always try to reserve a seat. And I did this time (D 88, I still remember). I find the seat and there is someone sitting there so I say ‘excuse me, I think you’re sitting in my seat.’ And the lady says loudly “there are no reserved seats.” I start fumbling in my pocket for my ticket with assigned seating, but before I can show her she follows up with “and I’m six months pregnant.” “Oh wow! You trumped me with that last one!” I say and leave to stand near the exits until a seat becomes available. I felt bad after the encounter. I felt like she thought I was trying to get one over on her and she was broadcasting this opinion.

I got in around 7pm today. The sun goes down very late in the summer so I took some photos of the harbour. It was full of floating boats and waves crashing into the barrier walls.

The only deli selling fresh vegetables closes at 4pm, so I had a light dinner, which is good for me.

Day 2

Today the entire harbor is dry! Today I learned that tides go in and out daily. Thank goodness! Otherwise I was worried about the harbor!

Day 3

I’m feeling refreshed. This is me at breakfast time:

I was going to go on a big hike to the south, but decided to stay in the harbor area and write postcards. On the way to the shop I learned that Mousehole has a bell-tower. Like a clock tower that chimes every hour. In Oxford – and even in Nottingham – there are church bells that ring on the hour/ on the quarter hour. But those are religious. This clock tower appears secular. At the post card shop I learned there is a Mobile Post Office that visits the village once a week for about an hour. Just my luck that it was here, then. So I bought stamps and took a selfie:

On the walk back, I passed a cottage called ‘Peppercorn.’ 1 I cannot understand this property.

The front looks to be a souvenir shop and/or art studio. There are loads of small figurines, trinkets, and hippy-symbols. The top of bi-door is usually open, the bottom usually closed. As it is located on a fairly high-traffic road, I thought it was a sort of stall to sale wares. But if you poke your head in, you will see a bed, a child lounging on it, and parents in the kitchen behind. I bought a rock from in front.

Around 5pm I was watching the tide come in (apparently, daily). Some people were wearing wet-suits (like surfers) and some just swim trunks. I struck up a conversation with a woman and asked about the wet suits. Turns out is it (1) for parents who are too lazy to apply sun-block to their kids and (2) to keep body temperature up. I looked out over the harbour – at this time of day it is also a beach. I decided I might want to take a dip. Why not? But then my brain immediately want to procrastination / scaredy-cat mode,2 But then I said to myself: “Fuck it. Lets do this now.” So I walked back, put on my swim-trunks, grabbed a towel and headed out.3 I made it about 10 feet before turning back. I see why they wear wet-suits.

Day 5

Boat ride!

While waiting for the boat to arrive at the doc (yes, I was actually early for once!) I saw lots of kids in wet-suits jumping from the barrier walls into the sea. They all said ‘hi’ to each other. Do they live here? Where do they go to school? Or are under-ten-year olds just naturally friendly to one another?

Day 6

  1. Today was sunny and everyone was out. Most have dogs.
  2. All the roads are also walk-ways. They are very narrow and bendy. Cars and people sometimes take turns.
  3. Seagulls (or cormorants?) are everywhere. I don’t mind their squawks, but I see how it would bother some.
  4. All the cottages have names, mostly nautical themed like “Pilot’s Cabin” or “Harbor End.” The cottage I am stay at is called “Bird House,” perhaps because there is a balcony on the top floor. I love it.

Random musings from a mouse:

I also discovered a crazy-cool foot-path. But not one like I’ve seen before. It was like something out of the TV show ‘Lost.’ Lush and green and overgrown and rushing water and seemingly inexplicable non-human-made-looking constructions.

It has been so long since I had a vacation (a.k.a. went on holiday). Before I left I was worried I might have forgotten how to ‘go on holiday.’ But I got back into it quickly. I really like it: it is like re-focusing all my attention on doing/ achieving things, but not for work. I basically always work on academic stuff (or work on things that I can convince myself are academic so as to really procrastinate e.g. ‘researching how to be more efficient at research.’ Ha!). And I always have a perpetual ‘to do’ list that seems to only get longer. But now my to do lists are things like: ‘post cards’; ‘figure out where to buy fresh vegetables’; book a boat ride to see local wildlife. A really, really nice change of pace!

Plus: right now I’m sitting on a balcony looking at an amazing view, with a cool breeze gently coming and going.

-Scott (currently a mouse)
